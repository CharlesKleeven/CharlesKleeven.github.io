    i just had a full speech in my head about the town i want to create: basically talking about how i could have easily just created a cute, quaint town with happy people, but wanted to create a town that meant a lot to me and came from my soul. for me, it’s like the difference between, say an isekai and a mundane slice of life manga. i used to think wild, fantastical worlds were amazing, and while I still enjoy such worlds, as I’ve grown over the years, my tastes have changed. I’ve learned I really enjoy super-ordinary slice-of-life manga. Although I really do enjoy the more dramatic slice-of-lifes, like the ones where one of the two main characters has a terminal disease or memory problems or something and the other main character doesn’t know, I’ve learned that my favorite are actually just the slice of life where nothing extreme happens. when i watch good mundane slice of life it’s like i’m able to notice and capture small, valuable moments and i’ve grown to love that. like how studio ghibli characters feel so human and the movies, such as my neighbor totoro, feel like i’m peering into someone’s life that’s sprinkled with fantasy. i do enjoy super cute, happy towns, but i guess i have an affinity towards towns like hmds because they’re just so normal. i’ve really learned to like the small things— i remember how i used to really dislike the workout dad who i thought was weird, while i really liked his beautiful wife and their cool kid. i also felt bad for that one tired dad and was annoyed by his little, obnoxious daughter who was always laughing. it was just the little things that made it feel so much more real. another big thing is that, and i can only speak for myself, when i play or read about a cute world where everyone is happy and gets along when i’m, y’know, a bit sad, i start thinking things like “i wish i lived in this world” but then it sometimes spirals into “why can’t the world be like this?” or “why couldn’t i have been born here” to even feeling like everyone is mocking me and my situation/feelings with their happiness. that’s not to say that i can’t play or read about closely, the less obvious things start to really shine once you pay attention. i guess the main difference for me, is that with a cute, happy little town i feel a strong sense of yearning and want, while with a mundane, slice-of-life town i’ve learned to appreciate, and although this difference is small, it’s a difference i still greatly value nonetheless.

Writing Tips

Writing Characters People Care About

Short recap:

  1. Don't just world build: what story is about (W) vs external plot events (w)
  2. Themes: the idea that the storyteller wants to get across; characters have conflicts but the theme wins
  3. Characters: must have arc and always be moving forward, whether up or down [good content here]
  4. Show don't tell
  5. Rise above: strong characters and stories have flaws, thus conflicts; just be believable, not realistic
  6. Visual basics: appeal; it's about invoking a reaction from the audience; also avoid visual cliches
  1. Some writers have this advice: you should have a playlist of your character’s favorite music, what they ate for breakfast that morning, their favorite color, but avoid the trap of world building so much that it's too late to tell your actual story:

Focus on the uppercase W: What your story is about! What makes us understand and care about characters, things like character arcs and themes.

Not the lower case w: all things in world building like the plot and the external events that happen.

example: Jaws

  • W: character overcoming the fear of water
  • w: 3 dudes who hunt a shark

THIS is how you create characters people care about. A plot, the action set-pieces, the external events that happen in a story aren’t enough to make us care about a character. But seeing them go through something and how they act and grow, that does. This relates to themes.

  1. Themes:

Not a word like revenge, rather a statement. Revenge is short-sighted and will come back to you. Theme is like the moral of a story, but not blatantly stated. It’s the idea that the storyteller wants to get across to you. Characters will come on both sides of this idea, maybe have some conflicts over this ideal and ultimately the storyteller chooses which side wins. The strongest stories use themes as a branching off point, as a spine for the rest of the story and ever idea either supports/relates to it or is trimmed off.

  1. Characters:

Characters need to move forward, have an arc. The way people will most potently care about your character is if they get to experience and see this character going through this arc firsthand. You can make a character tragic with a sad backstory or triumphant, and people care the most if they see them going through this firsthand. Seeing them start from one place and growing, either learning something or going through a change as a person, either postively or negatively. The more honest and self-actualized as a person, the more heartfelt a story and characters. Don’t let characters sit still. Don’t tell us the things that happened, show us.

  1. Show don’t tell:

Don’t write a long biography about your character, who their parents were, what powers they used to have all those things, then expect people to read it. Like, describing a dream isn’t fun. You need to make them experience it, like making a movie and getting them to watch and experience it.

  1. Rise above:

Rise above plausibility, and instead focus on what makes characters and stories so powerful in the first place. If you create strong characters, they’re going to have flaws. They’ll be prone to making mistakes, miscommunications and have conflicts over these. It doesn’t have to be realistic, just be believable.

  1. Visual Basics:

Especially if you’re designing and drawing characters, it’s important to make people care about them visually. You can make people take your characters at face value or you can subvert those expectations, make them not what they seem. Either way, you’re communicating something to them. Give appeal! (like baby features to make them adorable or ruggedly handsome are all appeal). It’s about invoking a reaction from your audience.

Visual Cliches to avoid:

  • Overly dark or brooding (wears all black, really sullen and downcast all the time)
  • Rainbow color schemes (using too many of them) in a character
  • Casts of characters that include but are not limited to anime aesthetics—this is where all characters are basically, roughly the same size and shape. All looks pretty much the same except for a change in hairstyle, hair color, eye color.

Other tips to make realistic characters:

  • birds of a feather flock together: characters should not be isolated from their own

    example: girls will flock together with girls, and asians with asians

    Note: not sure how relevant this is with children

General Learning Notes

Visuals

12 Principles of Animation

  1. Squash and stretch: elasticity. Degree of elasticity tells weight, energy.
  2. Anticipation: preparation for the main action. “Broadcasting intent”
  3. Staging: includes timing, pauses, focusing attention at one thing at a time, camera angles, etc. Purpose is to direct audience’s attention and make it clear what is of greatest importance in a scene: “Clarity of intent”
  4. Straight ahead/Pose to pose: animating as you go or drawing main poses and then drawing in between. Straight ahead is good for unpredictable things with laws of physics (e.g. fire, water drops, explosions, hair, tail). Pose to pose is better in general (keys, extremes, breakdowns).
  5. Follow through and overlapping action: drag, appendages move after the body has stopped. Overlapping action refers to the objects moving past the other body parts. Drag is the delay an appendage moves in relation to the main body part. When main body moves, tip of appendage should be last to catch up and move the farthest before settling.
  6. Slow in and slow out
  7. Arcs
  8. Secondary action: gestures that support the main action to add more dimension
  9. Timing: the personality and nature of an animation is greatly affected by the number of frames inserted between each main action. (Less drawings = fast. More drawings = slow); standard fps for movies is 24 fps. One drawing for each frame is ones. One drawing for every two frames is drawing on twos. Threes. Fours. ctc
  10. Exaggeration: doesn’t mean more distorted, rather more convincing! One way to judge how much to exaggerate is push exaggerations until they’re too much, then wind it back.
  11. Solid drawing: making forms feel like they’re in 3-dimensional space, with volume, weight, and balance. Try to draw characters roughly with 3d objects instead of squares, circles, etc. (2d). When drawing cleaner character design, try to overlap a lot. Without overlap, everything seems flat. Avoid symmetry. Instead of two straight lines, maybe one straight and one curve, or both offset curves. Avoid twinning.
  12. Appeal: characters should look appealing. Characters should be charismatic. Not just hero. Appeal doesn’t mean good-looking rather interesting or like-able. giving characters a dynamic design greatly boosts appeal: 1. Variety of shapes (give different characters different shapes instead of uniform) 2. proportions (exaggerate characters’ key proportions such as small for cute or hunched back for witch) 3. Keep it simple (animated characters must be drawn a billion times, so cut down on unnecessary information and design. Pick and choose key characteristics)

Pixel Art

  • dark with light on top shows depth/layers (e.g. dark and light leaves)

  • for atmospheric art, bring more depth through monochromatic palette

  • sub-pixeling animation: for subtle movement (e.g. breathing and trembling), useful to simulate shakings, rotations, wind effects, flickering lights, wobbling and jiggling, laughing, flicking, being stunned, shivering, and staggering mentions; more commonly used in idle animations

    • shifting colors basic principle
    • 12 principles of animations is very important
    • try to reuse existing colors
    • swimmming/bleeding pixel animation
    • not too good for facial features

  • fix jaggies: don't surround row of pixels with bigger ones (e.g. bad: 2-1-2)

  • staircases: keep each step consistent

  • line thickness: keep consistent, usually single or 2-pixel

  • outlines: selective outline with sources of light in mind (study: pokemon sprites)

  • anti-aliasing:

    • AA helps smooth edges; usually blends dark and light, but sometimes between 2 shades, too
    • to use or not to use, that is the question:
    • pros (AA): smooth curves on small sprite, necessary for large sprite, sub-pixeling animation
    • cons (AA): tedious, blurs tiny sprites,
    • pros (aliased): readable small sprites, limits colors, faster
    • cons(aliased): jagged linear, sharp, and block
    • how many blocks? ~length of line (less > more); how many colors? 1-2; 3 if confident and enough colors
    • longer steps == more shades of AA
    • 45° lines: convex curves [center is light, ends dark], concave curves [center is dark, ends light]
    • when fixing jaggies: fill large gaps with darker colors and smaller gaps with lighter colors
    • line weight: dark == thicker; light == thinner (e.g. thick/thin mouths and deep/shallow dimples)
    • banding == bad

  • colors: syosa puts dark colors and light colors together as much as possible; reusing same shadow colors looks more beautiful according to him

  • foreground is darker and background is lighter to show depth (e.g. scenery)

  • dither:

    • pros (dithering): good for limited colors, skies, vast backgrounds.
    • cons (dithering): rough, gritty, time-consuming, unnecessary texture, not suitable for sprites, useless for high colors, hinders animation, wobbling frames
    • pros (smooth): small sprites and tile sets, readable sprites, better for animation, soft painted or cel-shaded feel
    • cons (smooth): may look flat, doesn't do graidents well
    • generally, use dithering where cross hatching would usually be done

  • parallax scrolling:

    • the closer a piece of landscape, the faster it moves

    • 3 different types:

      1. following: all layers move in same direction, different speeds (e.g. rain, clouds)
      2. traveling: lyers move in opposite directions, scene makes the camera turn (e.g. car driving with scenery in the background)
      3. revolving: background loops, the front is frame by frame, camera revolves (e.g. camera revolving around the world where background is sky and front is all the different houses, trees, etc.)

  • particle effects (application examples):

    • dust or sand particles from running and jumping around
    • weather elements like rain, snow, fog, lightning, etc.
    • weapons and effects when fighting
    • generally good for powers and effects (like magic)

Audio/Sound

Reaper

Basic Features:

  • volume (white nob), mute (M), solo (S)
  • move audio freely by disabling snap (magnet icon above tracks)
  • fades: drag top right/left corner of audio snippet towards center to fade; right click fade to select different types of fades
  • cross fades: when overlapping audio clips, reaper creates a cross fade to make the audio flow more smoothly
  • pan: white knob next to volume; controls where audio is played (left and right ears) and double-click to center
  • normalize (right click audio clip): makes sound loudest possible without clipping
  • create a track with the applied FX (right click on audio —> apply track/take FX to items as new take)
  • send (make an empty track with an FX, then drag other send boxes, black boxes next to volume, to the empty FX track’s send box): basically “sends” FX down a chain of tracks. the sends route the tracks back to the FX
  • loop: select and loop with toggle button near the bottom, looks like circle next to red button

Sound effects (FX):

  • FX power button allows original audio to play when turned off
  • click and drag FX to different tracks to copy/paste the effect; same with individual effects if there are >1 effect in the FX
  • FX can be applied to specific audio clip and not just whole track
  • order of effects matters
  • frequency and equalizers (ReaEQ); changes volume of the frequencies, not changing the pitch of the frequencies
  • pitch shifting (ReaPitch)
  • compressor (Digital Versatile Compressor): essentially compresses the dynamics of an audio clip so that the louder sounds are softer and the softer sounds are louder
  • reverb (ReaVerb or ReaVerbate)
  • getting rid of background noise/hissngi (ReaFir): subtract. check the box for making noise profile and uncheck after background noise is played
  • shifting formant (ReaPitch): shifting down makes vocals sound like comes from a bigger person with a bigger throat and deeper voice. vise versa

Audio knowledge:

  • mono vs stereo: mono plays audio from one source (left and right ears together), stereo plays different audio into left ear and into right ear
  • all ambience should be stereo!
  • red tab: signifies audio clipping; beware! change volume to avoid clipping and check master mixer for clipping
  • envelopes: attack, decay, sustain, release
  • dynamics: things like softer footsteps and movement, louder sounds when being attacked/being more engaged

FMOD

Set up:

  • import FMOD unity package and install assets (Unity —> Assets —> Import Package …)
  • save FMOD project into main folder alongside Assets, Library, etc.
  • set Project Path for FMOD (Unity —> FMOD —> Edit Settings —> Browse [next to Studio Project Path]): click on the FMOD project file ending in .fspro
  • make sure to hit build (file —> build) on FMOD
  • events on Unity: find event with event browser (Unity —> FMOD —> Event Browser)

Using FMOD audio software:

  • create and name new event: 2D or 3D (2D is sound from the center and 3D depends on where something is in space and pans sound output)

  • drag and drop audio clip

  • assign event to a bank (right click —> Assign to Bank)

  • build (file —> build)

  • on Unity, find event (FMOD —> Event Browser)

  • effects (click on audio clip —> plus button near the bottom —> Add effect)

  • multi instrument (drag and drop multiple audio files into one track): plays one of the multiple audio tracks when played; randomizes sound

    good examples: varying footstep sounds (5-10 different sounds)

  • randomize (click on volume or pitch circle things after clicking on audio clip and “add modulation” then “random.” adjust white knobs on the right): randomizes volume or pitch every time the audio is played; similar to multi instrument use

  • along with add modulation, test out different things you can do, including AHDSR (attack/hold/decay/sustain/release)

FMOD Scripts and Coding:

  • Debug.Log();

    on a Unity script, use Debug.Log(); to test where actions are in a game.

    example: Debug.Log(“Goal!”); will show Goal! on Unity console when a goal is made while playing the game

    very useful for locating where sounds should be implemented

  • FMODUnity.RuntimeManager.PlayOneShot();

    find event in event browser. after clicking on the event, there is a copy icon in top-right corner of box that shows up. click on the icon and paste into the parentheses of the code, but include quotation marks. this just plays the sound when the line of code runs

    example: FMODUnity.RuntimeManager.PlayOneShot(“event:/Explosion”);

  • Script Listener: turn off “Audio Listener” component and include “Script Listener” component for good practice

  • FMOD.Studio.EventInstance stringNameEvent;

declare a string (ex: public string stringName;) use the line of code FMOD.Studio.EventInstance stringNameEvent

  • FMODUnity.RuntimeManager.CreateInstance();

    example code:

    public string stringName = “event:/StringName”; // sets string to FMOD event
FMOD.Studio.EventInstance stringNameEvent; //declares an event instance

void someMethod() 
{
stringNameEvent = FMODUnity.RuntimeManager.CreateInstance(stringName); //Creates instance
stringNameEvent.start(); // STARTS event
}

  • stringNameEvent.start(); and stringNameEvent.stop();

starts and stops event; can be used with empty parentheses or with added code (example below)

  • FMOD.Studio.STOP_MODE.ALLOWFADEOUT

a way to stop an event allow a fadeout. still required to make the fadeout in FMOD beforehand (i.e. won't apple a fadeout from just the code)

example code: stringNameEvent.stop(FMOD.Studio.STOP_MODE.ALLOWFADEOUT);

 

Cubase

Features

  • chord track: pencil in where you want chords, then click to place chords
  • chord pads: assign chords to pads
  • reverb and effects
  • VCA faders
  • audio detection (click audio clip —> Project —> Tempo Detection —> Analyze)
  • divide track list (top right icon)
  • change track colors for organization (highlight tracks —> click color icon at top)
  • folders (highlight tracks —> right click —> move selected tracks to new folder)
  • shift + scroll to navigate sideways
  • dynamics (looks like you’re drawing in fades)

Using Cubase:

  • don’t mix to 0db
  • create a template for better workflow

Writing music tips:

  • strong melody can be good

  • having a hook can be good

  • writing sections in this order: woodwinds, brass, percussion, keyboard, voice, strings

  • write each section from the highest to the lowest sounding instruments (specifically as follows):

    woodwinds: piccolos, flutes, oboes, clarinets, bassoons, saxophones (saxes can go before bassoons too)

    brass: horns, trumpets, trombones, euphonium, tuba

    percussion: timpani, high-to-low instruments, drum kit

    keyboard: celeste, harpsichord, piano, organ or synthesizer

    strings: violin 1, violin 2, viola, cello, double bass/contrabass; harp (unclear where)

Zoom H5

How to record:

  • make sure light above L and R are on/red (notice: for even audio, control nob values)
  • adjust top knob (overall volume) so that input does not clip
  • (if necessary: format SD card, which will erase all information — do this by hitting menu button and scrolling to SD card)
  • press menu button and click on folder; choose which folder to record to.

 

Writing Music

  • tempo:
  • beats > ~80bpm (average heart rate) tend to stimulate and excite, while beats < ~80bpm have calming effect
  • ideas:
  • sad person with happy exterior: happy vibes written in sad key
  • getting lost: have melody that does not return to home base
